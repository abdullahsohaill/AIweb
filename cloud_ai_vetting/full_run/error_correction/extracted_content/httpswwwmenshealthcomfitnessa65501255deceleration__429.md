NBA STARS ARE like sports cars: They both need good brakes. All well and good to hit 160  in your Audi on the Autobahn—but if you can’t decelerate back down to zero in a heartbeat, you’re in big trouble. For players like Luka Dončić, the 26-year-old LA Lakers megastar , controlling the pace of the game can make a difference between beating a defender to make space for a spot-up jumper or getting stuffed.
There's more to body control on the court than just speed and winning one-on-ones; if you don’t know how to brake, something’s going to—well— break. Normally when you pivot and cut on the field or court, explains Sports Medicine Physician Dr. Tom Cross, “The quadriceps and hamstrings co-contract to stabilize the [knee] joint.” That’s called eccentric force—the ability to absorb impact on a dime.
Good athletes like Dončić have it in spades. On the court, Dončić isn’t a speed demon—he's better known for piling up points (28.6 per game for his seven-season career) using his next-level IQ and skill—but he’s unquestionably a decel demon.
“His eccentric force is very good,” says Javier Barrio, the head of Dončić's performance team. “And his strength is off the charts really; he has really incredibly strong legs. Also his center of gravity is really very low. So when he’s in his position, when he gets low in his stance, it’s very difficult to move him, you know?”
That's what allows him to stop and change directions just a little bit faster than his defenders. And when you’re as deadly as Dončić from the field, that’s all the time you need. If good brakes prevent injury, exceptional brakes win games—sometimes championships. Decel has been recently identified as an under-recognized key to athletic greatness, not just in basketball but in basketball, volleyball, football, and soccer, too. If you’re an elite performer, chances are you have exceptional brakes.
But sometimes, in the heat of competition—when an athlete gets tired or distracted, for example—the body doesn't respond. "The neuromuscular system doesn't coordinate everything properly," says Dr. Cross. The muscles don’t turn on in time. Consequently, he says, “The skeletal system is exposed to this significant force that ruptures the ligament.” There goes your ACL, or your Achilles tendon—and possibly your competitive season along with it.
That’s why smart trainers spend lots of time teaching their athletic clients to absorb force as well as to produce it: to land as well as jump, decelerate as well as accelerate. Land poorly, and you might end up on injured reserve. Land right, and you’ll live to jump another day.
Deceleration may be a Luka Magic superpower, but it’s also something he works hard to hone. First, by ensuring that his glute muscles are active and strong—the better to stabilize the knee joint when you land—and second, by practicing landing and stepping movements, often with extra weight or resistance, so the muscles reliably jump to attention at the right moment.

## Deceleration Drills You Can Use

Try these four Luka-tested decel-moves to keep your joints safe—and shake off the big man at your next pick-up game:

## Bulgarian Glute Activator

How to Do It:
Loop a small exercise band around your thighs, just above your knees. Stand about two feet in front of a 12” box or bench, and place the top of your left foot on it. Standing upright, drive your left knee away from your right as far as possible, then return to the starting position. Perform a total of 12 reps, then repeat the move on the other side.

## Banded Jab Steps

How to Do It:
Affix a long exercise band to something sturdy, loop it around the back of your waist, and step backward so that there is tension on the band. From an athletic stance, take a short lunge-step forward with your left foot. As quickly as you can, push off the foot and return to the starting position. Alternate legs for a total of ten steps.

## Banded Lateral Triple-Threat

How to Do It:
Holding a medicine ball, assume an athletic stance with the same exercise band from the previous exercise around your waist. Turn 90 degrees to the anchor point so that the exercise band is pulling you to your right. Take a large, explosive step to your left with your left foot, and rotate your upper body into the triple threat position with the ball near your left hip. Return to the starting position. Perform a total of five reps, then repeat on the other side.

## Power Step-Up

How to Do It:
Stand behind a sturdy six-to-12-inch high box or bench. Place your right foot on the box. Driving hard through your right foot, jump as high as possible, landing with your left foot on the box and your right foot on the floor. Alternate legs for a total of ten jumps.
