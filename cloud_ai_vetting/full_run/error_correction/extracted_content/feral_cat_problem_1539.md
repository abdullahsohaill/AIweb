## Colonies of feral cats are out of control in Cambridge

## There has been a dramatic increase in the number of diseased and potentially dangerous strays on the streets of the city, according to local volunteers

How close are you to a hidden colony of wild abandoned cats right now?
If you’re reading this in Cambridge, then you could be a lot closer than you think, with the number of potentially diseased cats in the city rocketing in recent years.
Colonies are usually started when a stray mother cat gives birth to a litter of kittens, which can quickly become feral if they are not socialised within weeks.
If kitten aren't socialised within 10 weeks of being born, they can go completely feral
If these kittens grow up on the streets, scavenging from bins and alleyways, and aren’t neutered, then their numbers rapidly multiply.

### 'Nasty diseases'

Three years ago Cambridge Cats Protection were receiving “two to three” calls a week about feral colonies in and around the city, but now they’re busier than ever.
Volunteer Joanna Witt has been helping to rescue cats in Cambridge for six years.
The Cats Protection volunteers hunting for a feral colony
She told the News : “There’s definitely been an increase, because there is an increase in people abandoning their cats.
“They are not getting them neutered, and so they are vulnerable to a lot of nasty diseases.”
As well as taking in unwanted pets, the charity is increasingly spending time hunting down litters in the wild, a task which takes them the length and breadth of the city, and beyond.
Joanna said: “Generally you get them wherever there is a food source. It doesn’t have to be someone feeding them, it can be bins.
“We have done stuff right in the city at the colleges, we have had one in Arbury right in the centre of the houses, and there have been some out in the villages. They can literally pop up anywhere.”
More colonies of feral cats are being discovered across Cambridge

### A race against time

Once the Cats Protection team have been informed of a colony, it’s a race against time for them to get to the cats in time.
Joanna said: “People always seem to leave it too late, and then we can’t socialise the kittens.
“After the first few weeks we can’t tame them, that’s it, they are wild.
“We do occasionally rehome them, we get people to take on ferals if they have stables or farms, but taking them on is a big thing to commit to.”
She added: “It’s also the time it takes. You have to find out there is a colony, try to get in there, and it might not be somewhere that’s managed all the time, so you might have to go a couple of times.
“Then you need to work out how many cats are there, and what we are going to do, so it’s a lengthy process.
“One we found in one of the local villages took more or less a year.
“In the end it was really successful. They did find it and some of the cats were rehomed, but it’s a massive job and these ladies are working full time as well, we’re all volunteers.”

### Maggie’s tail

Maggie the cat
Now two years old, Maggie came into the care of Cats Protection as a stray with her two kittens, but it was only on closer inspection the team realised she was pregnant again.
Already settled in foster care, she’s now expecting her next litter when her current babies are only four months old.
While Maggie is friendly and has clearly had human contact in the past, her kittens could easily have all gone feral if they hadn’t gone into foster care.
Maggie was rescued from an address in Cambridge
Joanna said: “There is a small window of time when a kitten can be handled and socialised by humans and they will be a domesticated cat.
"The timing is crucial and there is only up to 10 weeks before they become feral but the ideal time to handle them is five to six weeks to really be successful with taming them.
“If one unneutered female cat has kittens, and you leave them, you could have a feral colony of up to 30 cats within a year.”
She added: “If you really love your cat it is kinder to have it neutered than to let it have a litter of kittens, there are too many unwanted cats and kittens in the world.
Maggie is now expecting a second litter
“Plus there are diseases that a cat that is allowed to mate can develop such as FIV which cannot be vaccinated against and feline leukaemia which is fatal. These diseases are not exclusive to feral cats!”

### Where are the colonies?

New freshers
Feral cats can make a home anywhere they have a warm place to stay and a steady supply of food, and have been known to set up in shops, restaurants and even schools.
Four years ago Cats Protection was called to Gonville and Caius College, where three stray kittens were found in a concrete basement.
After two attempts, and over six hours of waiting, the team was able to trap the mum and kittens, and take them in for assessment.
The kittens were adopted by new homes, while the mum was neutered and returned, to make sure she couldn’t start a new colony.
Just down the road three kittens were also found at Clare College, which were hand-reared and eventually rehomed.
A successful rescue from a feral rural colony
The ‘Cottenham Three’
A colony discovered in Cottenham also caused issues for the team, when they were called to help “three feisty female kittens who really didn’t want to be caught”.
But after giving the team the run-around in the village, they were eventually taking in for socialising, and have all now been provided with new homes.

### Why are stray numbers increasing?

Maggie and her litter- who were rescued just in time to prevent them becoming feral
Joanna and the team are spending more of their time tracking down ferals than ever before, a trend which has been growing for years.
Joanna told the News growing financial pressures meant more people were abandoning cats than ever before.
“It’s about money,” she said.
“And sometimes people in multi-cat homes get a bit embarrassed. They feel no-one will help them if they have too many, so they are letting them go out, and not checking where they are going, and before you know it a colony has formed.”

For those struggling to care for a cat, acting quickly is the key thing to prevent the problem spiralling out of control.
Joanna said: “There is no point not helping, as if you don’t help it will just make a headache in the long run.”
In a bid to combat the rise in ferals, Cats Protection is offering discounted neutering to anyone on benefits or a low income.
Some of the Cats Protection volunteers
On February 27, as part of World Spay Day, cat-owners can get their pet neutered for £5, which the charity hopes will prevent the risk of cats raising litters in the wild.
