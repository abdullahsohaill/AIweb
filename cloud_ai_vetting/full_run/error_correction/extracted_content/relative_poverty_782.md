## Relative Poverty Definition

What is relative poverty ? Generally this occurs when people are unable to obtain the resources needed to achieve the average standard of living compared to others in their community. The UN, the World Bank, individual countries, and many other entities each have their own relative poverty definitions. Setting a firm financial metric on relative poverty is difficult, since the average standard of living varies depending on the country or even area of a country someone lives in. In addition, relative poverty changes as the wealth and cost of living in an area increases or decreases over time. It is considered, however, the easiest way to determine an individual country's level of poverty .

### Relative Poverty Examples

Relative poverty looks different depending on where you are in the world. For example, for indigenous people living a nomadic lifestyle, relative poverty could mean a family has significantly less cattle or other animals than others in their community, while for people living in a city in a developed country, relative poverty could mean a family must rely on public transportation instead of being able to own a car. Other examples include a single mother of two with an income of $19,000 per year, a person on a fixed income such as social security, and unemployed people drawing unemployment benefits while looking for new employment. A common relative poverty example is to think of a person who is able to provide for their basic needs, such as food and shelter, but unable to obtain other standard things the people around them have that make life easier and more enjoyable. These things may include:
- Personal transportation, such as a car or bicycle
- Entertainment, such as movie or concert tickets
- Eating out at restaurants
- Internet access
- Safe housing
- Higher education

## Absolute vs Relative Poverty

Absolute poverty is more extreme than relative poverty. In this condition, a person is unable to provide for their basic needs, including food and shelter. The World Bank defines absolute poverty using the International Poverty Line (IPL), which as of 2015 stands at $1.90 income per day. The UN World Summit, on the other hand, does not use a financial metric, and instead defines it as ''a condition characterized by severe deprivation of basic human needs.'' The World Bank definition is often criticized, since it is not applicable equally everywhere. For instance, in the United States, absolute poverty is much higher than $1.90 a day since the cost of living is so high. ## Causes of Relative Poverty

There are many factors that contribute to relative poverty. Some are unavoidable consequences of having been born into a particular location or circumstance, and some are more avoidable consequences of personal choices.

### Unemployment

In the United States, over 18 million people between the ages of 16 and 24 were unemployed in 2020 as per the US Bureau of Labor Statistics. Unemployment creates a loss of income, which directly leads to both absolute and relative poverty. Some people, although actively seeking employment, are unable to find a job that pays a living wage , which is the amount needed to meet one's basic needs. Job availability can also be affected by natural disasters and exploitation of resources, both of which will reduce employment opportunities in certain areas and industries. Another problem is that, in the United States, there are many more jobs that pay poverty-level wages than there are jobs paying high wages. This can cause underemployment , where a person is forced to take a job they are overqualified for (which likely pays much less than a job they would be qualified for) because no other jobs are available.

### Lack of Education

Around the world, there are many barriers to education for those in poverty. Some families may be unable to afford to send their children to school because they need their labor for the family farm or business to survive. The less education a person has, the less likely they are to find a job. Without basic skills like reading and math, finding a job that pays a living wage can be nearly impossible. In addition, those who are able to finish grade school, but lack the resources for attending college, can also face barriers to good employment.

### Lack of Access to Services

There are many services that can affect a person's ability to maintain a sufficient income. A person with small children will find it difficult to regularly attend a job if they cannot afford childcare for the times they are gone. Similarly, a person without a means of personal transportation will be limited to jobs they can either walk to or find reliable public transportation to. An inability to obtain these services will often directly affect income opportunities.

### Health Complications

A person with a chronic disease or disability will be extremely limited on the types of jobs they can do, and if none are available near them, will be forced to depend on other forms of income. Also, a healthy person who suddenly is diagnosed with a prolonged illness may lose the job they had if their employer decides it would be more profitable to replace them than to wait for them to return. A parent who must care for a child with health issues may find it more difficult to obtain affordable childcare, thus threatening their income.

### Changing Industry Structure

Many industries are replacing low-skilled jobs with automation, which decreases the demand for low-skilled workers. This marginalization of low-skilled workers leaves individuals with fewer options for employment. They often lack the resources for education to raise their skills. Some industries are also utilizing part-time employment more than full-time employment, since they view part-time employees as more profitable. They are not required to provide benefits such as healthcare to a part-time employee, which gives their employees added financial burdens and increased risk of relative poverty.
As industries move toward automation, people are left with less employment opportunities.

### Climate Change

As droughts, fires, flooding, and severe storms become more common around the world, many people are at risk of being pushed into poverty. Many of the world's most vulnerable people, who depend on trades such as hunting and farming for their livelihood, live in the areas most affected by climate change currently. As they lose access to water, have their resources destroyed by disasters, and are forced out of areas due to the changing climate and weather patterns, they are unlikely to easily find employment or new areas to move to.
Droughts caused by climate change can be prolonged, leading to a decreased supply of water and forced relocation.

### Discrimination and Inequality

Relative poverty disproportionately affects people from minority groups, including those based on race, ethnicity , and gender. Women and people of color often face more difficulty in obtaining living wage jobs because of stereotypes, prejudices, and significant wage gaps. In some countries, minority groups are denied citizenship, face extra restrictions, and deal with social and cultural stigmatization, all factors which act as barriers to a good income.

## Consequences of Relative Poverty

The consequences of relative poverty are wide-ranging and encompass not only the physical aspects of life, but also social and emotional aspects. These consequences can include:
- Lack of access to health insurance, which means that minor illnesses and injuries can become much more serious
- Lack of access to quality food, which can contribute to obesity and other health problems
- Increased mental health issues, since families in relative poverty are under large amounts of stress and may lack access to support systems
- Lower life expectancy
- Worse outcomes for children, who experience more stress than their peers and often lack access to quality education
- Risk of social exclusion due to being unable to participate in social events that are cost prohibitive
- Lower self-esteem

## Concepts of Relative Poverty

### Primary vs Secondary Poverty

Primary poverty means that even if a family spent all of their money only on absolute necessities, they still would not have enough to meet their basic needs. This could mean choosing between paying the rent or keeping the electricity on, rather than choosing between eating out or eating at home. Secondary poverty , on the other hand, means that if a family spent all of their money wisely, they would be able to meet their basic needs. However, poor spending choices have put them in a position where they cannot meet those needs. This could mean choosing to go on a vacation instead of paying rent.
Wise money management is key to avoiding secondary poverty.

### Persistent and Long-Term Poverty

Persistent poverty , or long-term poverty, means that a family or individual has been living below the poverty line for at least three years in a row. The consequences of poverty are intensified when endured over long periods of time, especially for children. Additionally, once poverty has become persistent, it is much more difficult to break out of the cycle and increase the standard of living. Without intervention, persistent poverty can become generational, negatively influencing future generations and giving them a starting point well behind their peers.

## How to Measure Poverty

Given that the standard of living and average income vary so greatly around the world, it can be difficult to decide on a system for measuring poverty. Different ideas about what poverty is have led to a number of different suggestions for how to measure it. For example, defining poverty as having no access to food and shelter will lead to a different measure than defining it as lacking access to social and cultural experiences as well.

### Relative Approach

The relative approach measures poverty by comparing a person's income to the average standard of living in a given location. Generally, anyone with an income of 50-60% or less compared to the average income in their country is considered in poverty under this approach. This metric identifies those suffering from both relative and absolute poverty.

### Absolute Approach

The absolute approach measures poverty by determining whether basic needs have been met. The World Bank's International Poverty Line of $1.90 a day is a good example of this kind of metric. This approach will identify those suffering from absolute poverty, but will exclude those suffering from relative poverty.

### Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI)

The Multidimensional Poverty Index , or MPI, was developed to acknowledge that poverty includes more than simply the traditional financial concerns. Instead of looking only at money, it also includes health, education, and living standards when determining whether a family or individual is in poverty. It looks at factors like nutrition, child mortality, years of education, sanitation, access to drinking water, and assets to get a more complete picture of a family's situation. This method is much more likely to identify people suffering from relative poverty in addition to those suffering from absolute poverty, since it captures more than simply monetary issues.

## Lesson Summary

There are several different types of poverty , each with their own unique causes and consequences.
- Relative poverty is a condition where basic needs may be met, but an average standard of living compared to peers is not.
- Absolute poverty is a condition where basic needs, such as food and shelter, are not met.
- Primary poverty is a condition where there is not enough money to meet basic needs.
- Secondary poverty is a condition where there would be enough money for basic needs if it were managed correctly.
- Persistent poverty occurs when poverty lasts for three or more consecutive years.
Relative poverty has a number of causes that contribute to negative outcomes, including:
- Unemployment
- Lack of education
- Lack of access to services
- Health complications, which can be a consequence as well as a cause of poverty
- Changing industry structures
- Climate change
- Discrimination and inequality
Measuring poverty can be done through a number of different approaches, each based on a different idea of what poverty is.
- The relative approach measures poverty as a percentage of the average income of others in a given area.
- The absolute approach measures poverty by whether basic needs are met or not.
- The Multidimensional Poverty Index measures poverty by including health, education, and living standards in addition to monetary issues.

## Relative Poverty Definition

Relative poverty is the condition in which people lack the minimum amount of income needed in order to maintain the average standard of living in the society in which they live. Relative poverty is considered the easiest way to measure the level of poverty in an individual country. Relative poverty is defined relative to the members of a society and, therefore, differs across countries. People are said to be impoverished if they cannot keep up with the standard of living as determined by society.
Relative poverty also changes over time. As the wealth of a society increases, so does the amount of income and resources that the society deems necessary for proper conditions of living. For example, if you were a family of four (two adults and two children) living in America in 1963 with a yearly income less than $3,100, you would have been living in relative poverty. By 1992, this amount had increased to $14,228 a year.

## What Causes Relative Poverty?

There are several causes of relative poverty, including:
From 2010-2014, the unemployment rate in the United States was over eight percent. This means that over 18 million Americans over the age of 16 did not have jobs. This lack of employment increases your chances of living in poverty.
The less education you have, the higher your chances of being unemployed, which in turn increases the likelihood that you will live in poverty.
Poor health is not only a cause of poverty, but it is also a consequence of poverty. People living in poverty suffer greater levels of physical and mental illness than those who do not. Poor health also weakens impoverished communities, which prevent many people from working and earning an income. For example, an infectious disease may spread across an impoverished community, killing many. Since these communities do not have access to adequate health care services, they cannot be treated and the cycle of poverty continues.
Affordable services, such as low-cost child care and affordable transportation to and from work, are essential in preventing and reducing poverty, especially among families with limited income. Without access to affordable services, some people will not be able to fully participate in social, academic and occupational activities. This in turn can lead to poverty.
Due to the decline of manufacturing in the United States, there is a lack of quality employment for individuals who possess low or outdated skills.
There is a large number of jobs available in the United States that only pay poverty-level wages or only offer a small number of work hours (i.e. 20 hours per week) so employers do not have to pay employee benefits. However, the pay of the top ten percent of the labor market has steadily increased.

## Examples of Relative Poverty

Let's look at some examples of relative poverty:
- A family of four whose yearly income is $20,000.
- A person who has just enough money to pay their bills and a bus pass, but no extra money for anything else, i.e. movie tickets or community events.
- A family whose yearly income of $22,000 is half of that of the other people who live in their community.
- A person who is unemployed and lives off a $455-a-month social security check.

Relative poverty refers to the lack of income needed to maintain the average standard of living in your society. Causes of relative poverty include unemployment, poor health, and inequalities within the labor market. An example of someone in relative poverty is a person who does not have a job and relies on government assistance to maintain.
