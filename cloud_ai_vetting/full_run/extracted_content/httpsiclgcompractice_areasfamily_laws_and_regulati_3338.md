### 1. Divorce

1.1       What are the grounds of jurisdiction for divorce proceedings? For example, residence, nationality, domicile, etc.?
In Japan, the Personal Status Litigation Law (“PSLL”) provides the rules for international jurisdiction of divorce proceedings.
Article 3-2 of the PSLL articulates that the following divorce cases will be handled by a Japanese court:
- when the address of the defendant is in Japan;
- when the nationalities of both parties to the divorce are Japanese; and
- when the last common address of the parties to the divorce was located in Japan and the plaintiff has his or her address in Japan; or there are found to be special circumstances because of which conducting a trial and reaching a judicial decision in a Japanese court will lead to achieving equity between the parties or ensure a fair and speedy trial, such as if the defendant is missing or if the final and binding judicial decision of the divorce that was filed in the country where the defendant has his or her address is not effective in Japan.
The above Article 3-2 came into force on April 1, 2019 as result of the amendment of the PSLL and it is rather new.  Before this amendment, under what circumstances the Japanese court had its jurisdiction over the international divorce cases was not articulated by law, and various interpretative theories have been developed in academic studies as well as in respect of judicial precedents.  The amendment as above was enacted mainly based on judicial precedents.
1.2       What are the grounds for a divorce? For example, is there a required period of separation; can the parties have an uncontested divorce?
In addition to the above, court mediation is another way to agree on divorce.  A party can apply for mediation at a family court.  There will be three members in the panel, and one member of the mediation panel must be a judge.  Such mediation at the court is another way for divorce by mutual agreement without a litigation and judicial judgment.  However, the official mediation record will ultimately be created by the court clerk, which has an important effect under Japanese law, and a party will often retain his or her counsel for the divorce mediation to protect his or her custodial or economical rights as well as to understand the legal implications of the outcome of the divorce to come.
In Japan, there are five grounds for a divorce articulated by Article 770(1) of the Civil Code (“CC”):
- a spouse has committed adultery;
- a spouse was wilfully abandoned by the other spouse;
- it is not clear whether a spouse is dead or alive for three or more years;
- a spouse is suffering from severe mental illness and there is no prospect of recovery; or
- there is any other material cause making it difficult to continue the marriage.
The last ground is normally interpreted as the same as “irrecoverable breakup of the marriage”.
If there is a history of domestic violence, criminal actions, unreasonable failure to work, serious wasteful habits, unreasonable denial of sexual intercourse, serious conflict with the spousal family members, difference in personal characteristics and lovelessness, the last ground will be recognised to have been established by the court.
In addition, the length of the period of separation is considered a very important factor in establishing the last ground, but separation is not required for divorce under Japanese law.
Under Japanese case law, the husband or wife who is responsible for the irrecoverable breakup of the marriage cannot win the divorce in the litigation if he or she wants a divorce.  This is often referred to among practitioners as the “defence of the responsible spouse”.  However, on September 2, 1987, the Supreme Court held that he or she could be awarded divorce if the length of separation is long enough, considering the ages of the parties and the duration of cohabitation and dependence of children and so on.  The Supreme Court held that unless there are special circumstances in which the divorce will cause extreme mental, social, or economic hardship, and granting the divorce would be contrary to social justice, the divorce will be awarded after a substantially long separation.
On May 17, 2024, the Law for Partial Revision of the Civil Code, etc. (Law No. 33 of 2024) (“New Civil Code”) was enacted.  This law clarifies the responsibilities of parents regarding the upbringing of their children in order to secure the interests of their children in the face of divorce, etc., and revises laws regarding parental rights and custody, child support, parent-child exchanges, adoption and division of property, etc.  The provision of item (4) of the above was deleted by the New Civil Code, which will come into force no later than May 24, 2026.
1.3       In the case of an uncontested divorce, do the parties need to attend court and is it possible to have a “private” divorce, i.e. without any court involvement?
As described above, divorce by an agreement is possible in Japan, and Japanese parties only have to file a notice of divorce to the head of municipality.
However, such divorce may not be recognised outside Japan and foreigners must be careful before utilising such divorce.
The uncontested divorce of foreign couples in Japan is often achieved by court mediation as explained above.  If any party is from a jurisdiction where mutual consent divorce without court involvement is not accepted, the parties have to apply for such mediation to get a divorce.
An adjudication of divorce by a family court judge can be used for foreigners in this regard.  Article 284(1) of the Domestic Relations Case Procedure Act (“DRCPA”) provides this adjudication, which can solve the problem.  The adjudication will be issued based on the mutual agreement for divorce expressed to the relevant judge and will become final two weeks after the delivery of the certified copy of such ruling to parties in accordance with Articles 287, 286(1)(2) and 279(2) of the DRCPA.
Most foreign embassies in Japan provide certain information to foreigners in connection with the divorce procedure in Japan.
1.4       What is the procedure and timescale for a divorce?
Divorce by an agreement is easy and very quickly completed.  The parties must file the divorce papers with the signatures of the parties to the head of municipality.
For judicial divorce, a party must file for mediation at the court before filing a divorce trial in accordance with Article 257(1) of the DRCPA.  This is called Mediation First Principle.
If the parties reach an agreement for divorce in family court mediation, the parties can divorce by mediation.  The normal length of this procedure will be approximately three to eight months.
If they cannot reach an agreement at the family court mediation, a party who wants to be awarded divorce can file a divorce trial.  In this litigation procedure, if the court recognises the establishment of one of the above grounds for divorce, the plaintiff should be awarded divorce.  In cases where there is a complicated issue regarding asset division or custodial dispute or responsibility for the ground between parties, it often takes more than one year for the trial, and cross-examination of parties will be required.
After enforcement of the New Civil Code, agreement for joint custody after divorce will be possible for the first time in Japanese law.
1.5       Can a divorce be finalised without resolving other associated matters? For example, children and finances.
No, with regard to custody.  If the parties contest for the parental authority of a minor, the person who should be awarded sole parental authority must be determined by an agreement or judgment at the time of divorce (see Article 819(1) and (2) of the CC).
Prior to the divorce, if the parent who lives with a minor is contested, the court will give an adjudication for physical custodian ( kango sha ), which means the parent who normally lives with the minor.  If a parent is awarded such custodian, such parent is likely to be awarded sole parental authority by the order of divorce trial.
In practice, finances such as distribution of property and child support can be agreed or ordered later than the time of divorce.
However, Japanese law states that the parents should agree on child support and visitation at the time of mutual agreement of divorce.  Article 766(1) and (2) of the CC state the following:
If parents divorce by agreement, the issues that need to be determined in connection with child custody, such as who will have custody over the child, visitation and other contact between the father or mother and the child, and the sharing of expenses that custody of the child requires, are determined in that agreement.  The child’s interests must be given the highest priority in the consideration of these issues in such case.
If the agreement referred to in the preceding paragraph is not reached or if discussions toward such an agreement cannot be held, the issues referred to in the preceding paragraph are determined by the family court.
1.6       Are foreign divorces recognised in your jurisdiction? If so, what are the procedural requirements, if any?
Yes.  Foreign divorces are recognised if such foreign divorce order is final and meets all the conditions provided by Article 118 of the Code of Civil Procedure as follows:
- jurisdiction of the foreign court is recognised under laws, regulations, conventions or treaties;
- the defeated defendant has received a service (excluding a service by publication or any other service similar thereto) of summons or order necessary for the commencement of the suit, or has appeared without receiving such service;
- the contents of the judgment and the court proceedings are not contrary to public policy in Japan; and
- a mutual guarantee exists.
As to item (1), there is no statutory provision on the rules of international allocations of adjudicatory jurisdiction under Japanese law.  It is generally accepted among scholars that the way to determine direct jurisdiction, which means the method of determining the exercise of Japanese judicial power over each litigation, should be applied to the way to determine the jurisdiction of foreign judgments in connection with item (1) as above.
As to item (2), there are cases where the service of summons and complaints were mailed to the defendant in Japan without annexing Japanese translations.  In such cases, the service did not satisfy item (2) above and the divorce orders were not recognised under Japanese law.
Item (3) is the normal requirement for any agreements, including legal arrangements, to be valid under Japanese law.
Item (4) is the requirement for reciprocity.
1.7       Does your jurisdiction allow separation or nullity proceedings?
Under Japanese law, the obligation for cohabitation of parties is stipulated, and there is no legal separation.  However, it is normally easy for parties to end the actual cohabitation by simply moving out.
The nullification of marriage is articulated by Article 742 of the CC, which stipulates that marriage shall be void under the following conditions:
- one of the parties has no intention to marry due to mistaken identity or other cause; or
- the parties do not lodge notification of marriage; provided, however, that the effect of marriage shall not be prevented merely because notification was not given in the form prescribed in paragraph (2) of Article 739 of the CC.
As to rescission of marriage, Article 744 provides that:
- either of the parties, their relatives, or a public prosecutor may make a claim to the family court to rescind the marriage if it violates the provisions of Articles 731 to 736 inclusive; provided, however, that a public prosecutor may not claim this after the death of one of the parties; and
- a spouse or previous spouse of a party to the marriage that violates the provisions of Article 732 or 733 may claim for rescission of marriage.
Article 731 provides for the rule of marriage age, and Article 732 provides for the prohibition of bigamy.
Articles 733 to 736 regard the following matters:
- Prohibition regarding the period between divorce and remarriage.
- Prohibition of marriage between close relatives.
- Prohibition of marriage between lineal relatives by affinity.
- Prohibition of marriage between adoptive parent and child.
1.8       Can divorce proceedings be stayed if there are proceedings in another country?
Even if divorce proceedings in another country are ongoing, the Japanese court would not deny its jurisdiction as long as the Japanese court has jurisdiction under Japanese conflict law.
After a foreign court has rendered a final judgment and such foreign judgment has been recognised as valid under Japanese procedural law, the Japanese court would dismiss the ongoing trial in Japan since the parties have been divorced under Japanese law and the ongoing Japanese proceeding no longer has merit.

### 2. Finances on Divorce

2.1       What financial orders can the court make on divorce?
The court can make orders regarding the distribution of property (Article 768 of the CC) and child support payments for child maintenance purposes (Articles 771 and 766(1) of the CC).
2.2       Do matrimonial regimes exist and do they need to be addressed by the court on divorce? Is there a default matrimonial regime?
A matrimonial regime is a marital property system and exists under Japanese law.
Section 3 of the CC, Part IV, is titled the marital property system.  Article 760 stipulates that a husband and wife share the expenses that arise from their marriage, in consideration of their assets, income, and all other circumstances.  This is an obligation of the marital cost sharing during the marriage, which will be terminated upon divorce under Japanese law.
As to the assets of a husband or wife, Article 762 provides that:
- property owned by one party before marriage and property obtained in the name of one party during the marriage is separate property (meaning property that one party to a marriage owns independently of the other party); and
- property that does not clearly belong to either husband or wife is presumed to be held in co-ownership.
Item (ii) creates a default matrimonial regime; however, even if one party owns assets under his or her name and such is deemed separate property under the above provision, such assets will be distributed at the time of divorce by distribution of property under Japanese statutory law and case law in accordance with Article 768 of the CC.
Article 768 prescribes the authority of the court for division of property.  If no agreement between the parties is reached with respect to the division of property, the family court will handle the division.  In this case, the relevant family court determines whether the parties should be subject to a division of property and the value and method of the division, taking into consideration the value of the property that the parties obtained together through their combined efforts and all other circumstances.
2.3       How does the court decide what financial orders to make? What factors are taken into account?
As stated above, Article 768 is applied for the distribution of property upon divorce.  If the parties agree on how to distribute the property, the court would not be involved and such agreement is final.  If both parties fail to agree on the distribution, the family court will determine the distribution, considering the value of property and efforts by the parties in connection with the property at the time of divorce or within two years after divorce.
Distribution of property is often thought to have three elements.  The first element is the distribution of assets acquired during marriage, second is compensation for the mental distress caused by the divorce itself, and third is post-divorce spousal maintenance.
However, the first element holds the most substantial value in cases, and the judge will normally conclude that such property be distributed equally to each party, except for in certain special cases.  The second element will be disregarded since the court would order the separate consolation money to be paid if the breakdown of the marriage is attributed to one party.  The third element will be applied only under exceptional circumstances, such as where the property distribution is very limited and one party’s life after divorce will be extremely financially difficult, and no means of distribution would be fair.
2.4       Is the position different between capital and maintenance orders? If so, how?
Under Japanese law, one spouse is required to support the other only before divorce.
As stated above, the distribution of property upon divorce can be used as a means of spousal financial support under very exceptional circumstances.
2.5       If a couple agrees on financial matters, do they need to have a court order and attend court?
No, they do not.
However, if the distribution method is complicated or enforcement should be secured, the parties should prepare for the written agreement following the advice of his or her lawyers.
A notarial deed is often used to secure the enforcement of agreements relating to property or maintenance.  Court mediation ( choutei ) can be another way to secure the enforcement of a mutual agreement.
2.6       How long can spousal maintenance orders last and are such orders commonplace?
In Japan, there is no spousal maintenance obligation after divorce.
However, as far as the parties are legally married, maintenance is an obligation no matter how long the parties are separated.  For this reason, a party, normally a woman, who is awarded maintenance payments may not agree to the divorce without being wholly satisfied with the distribution of property.
2.7       Is the concept of matrimonial property recognised in your jurisdiction?
With regard to matrimonial property, in Japan, a separation of property regime is applied, and property acquired before and during the marriage is each spouse’s own separate property if the asset is obtained in the name of such party; however, property that does not clearly belong to either husband or wife is presumed to be held in co-ownership.
2.8       Do the courts treat foreign nationals differently on divorce? If so, what are the rules on applicable law? Can the court make orders applying foreign law rather than the law of the jurisdiction?
In Japan, the governing law regarding divorce is prescribed by Article 27 of the Act on General Rules for Application of Laws (“AGRAL”) and if the husband or wife is a Japanese national who has their habitual residence in Japan, their divorce shall be governed by Japanese law.
If not, the divorce shall be governed by the national law of the husband and wife if their national law is the same.  If it is not same, the law of the habitual residence of the husband and wife will be applied if the habitual residence is the same.  If the habitual residence is not the same, for example, the American wife lives in Tokyo and the Chinese husband lives in Singapore, the law of the place most closely connected with the husband and wife will be applied in accordance with Articles 27 and 25 of the AGRAL.  Such connection would be handled by the relevant court judge, if the trial is ongoing, to determine the governing law.
2.9       How is the matrimonial home treated on divorce?
The matrimonial home is not treated as a special asset under Japanese law but as one item of assets for distribution.
2.10       Is the concept of “trusts” recognised in your jurisdiction? If so, how?
There is no recognition of family trusts under Japanese law.
2.11       Can financial claims be made following a foreign divorce in your jurisdiction? If so, what are the grounds?
The recognition of financial claims made following a foreign divorce are treated in the same manner as the foreign judgment mentioned in question 1.6 above.
2.12       What methods of dispute resolution are available to resolve financial settlement on divorce, e.g. court, mediation, arbitration?
In Japan, the parties can agree on financial matters by court mediation or seek the relevant judgment through litigation at the family court as to spousal support, child support and property distribution.

### 3. Marital Agreements

3.1       Are marital agreements (pre- and post-marriage) enforceable? Is the position the same if the agreement is a foreign agreement?
The CC provides a statutory marital property system, and parties to the marriage can conclude a prenuptial property agreement before marriage (Article 758 of the CC).  If the contents of the agreement depart from the statutory property system, the agreement may not be asserted against the successor in title of the husband or wife or against a third party unless it is registered before the marriage is registered.  Between the parties, the agreements will be enforceable if the contents are not against Japanese public policy (Article 756 of the CC).
As to a foreign agreement on marital property under a foreign law, such agreement may be asserted against a third party in good faith only when the contents of the agreement are registered in Japan (Article 26(4) of the AGRAL).  The governing law of such agreement is prescribed by Article 26(2) of the AGRAL and the parties can choose one of the following:
- the law of the country where either the husband or wife has nationality;
- the law of the habitual residence of either the husband or wife; or
- as to real property, the law of the place where the real property is situated.
3.2       What are the procedural requirements for a marital agreement to be enforceable on divorce?
Under Japanese law, marital agreements are concluded only before marriage (Article 755 of the CC).  The agreements are enforceable if the contents of the agreements are not against the public policy of Japan (Article 90 of the CC).  If the agreements have established special norms different from the Japanese statutory marital property system, the contents cannot be asserted against the successor in title of the husband or wife, or a third party (Article 756 of the CC).
3.3       Can marital agreements cover a spouse’s financial claims on divorce, e.g. for maintenance or compensation, or are they limited to the election of the matrimonial property regime? Can they deal with financial claims regarding children, e.g. child maintenance?
Yes, they can.
If a party has entered into a mutual agreement in order to establish a special arrangement, the contents cannot be asserted against the successor of the husband or wife, or a third party (Article 756 of the CC).  Such agreement can cover a spouse’s financial claims or compensation on divorce.  In addition, the agreement can cover financial claims regarding children, such as child maintenance.

### 4. Cohabitation and the Unmarried Family

4.1       Do cohabitants, who do not have children, have financial claims if the couple separate? What are the grounds to make a financial claim?
No.  There is no statutory requirement for the division of property for unmarried cohabitees.  Under special circumstances, Article 768 of the CC is applied mutatis mutandis to cohabitants under case law.  If they are deemed to have the intention of getting married but have not yet filed the formal registration, they will be known as de facto spouses and they may have financial claims.
4.2       What financial orders can a cohabitant obtain?
As mentioned above, case law admits such orders.
4.3       Is there a formal partnership status for cohabitants (for example, civil partnerships, PACS)?
No, there is not.
In Japan, 70% of Japanese municipal bodies such as wards or cities have established a partnership system for same-sex parties.  The effect of the partnership is, however, limited to circumstances in which they are treated in the same way as a married couple, mainly in connection with public bodies such as public hospitals or public residential accommodations.
4.4       Are same-sex couples permitted to marry or enter other formal relationships in your jurisdiction?
No, same-sex couples are not yet permitted to marry under Japanese law.  Some practitioners believe Article 768 of the CC for married parties should be applied mutatis mutandis to same-sex couples who want to live in the same way as a married couple.
On February 14, 2019, four lawsuits were simultaneously filed in the district courts of Sapporo, Tokyo, Nagoya and Osaka seeking the right for same-sex couples to marry.
On September 5, 2019, another lawsuit was filed in Fukuoka district court by a same-sex couple.
Since such couples cannot marry in Japan, they are not entitled to certain important rights afforded to married couples, such as inheritance rights and rights to raise children with legal joint parental authority.  The lawsuits handle constitutional rights in this regard.
In May 2023, Nagoya district court ruled that lack of legal recognition of same-sex marriage is unconstitutional, followed by the Sapporo district court ruling.
Current laws in Japan do not even provide a framework to protect the relationships of same-sex couples, and the Nagoya ruling stated that this violates Article 14 of the Japanese Constitution, regarding the right to equality, and Article 24, which guarantees the freedom of marriage.
The Tokyo and Fukuoka district court judgments admit that the current legal framework of Japanese civil law does not properly protect minorities in connection with Japanese constitutional rights regarding Article 24, paragraph (2).  These judgments did not state that the current framework is concluded in violation of the Japanese Constitution since the Japanese Diet, as the lawmaker, has the authority to make specific legal frameworks with discretion.  The Osaka judgment said the current legal framework is not against constitutional rights.
The first high court ruling was made in April 2024 in Sapporo and it upheld the original ruling and admitted that the current framework is in violation of Article 24.  It is expected that such outcome will add pressure on the Japanese government to take measures to protect the rights of sexual minorities.

### 5. Child Maintenance

5.1       What financial claims are available to parents on behalf of children within or outside of marriage?
Under Japanese law, the parent who does not reside with the dependent child has an obligation to pay child maintenance to the other parent who resides with the child both within and outside of marriage.  Parents have an obligation to support their dependent child.
5.2       How is child maintenance calculated and is it administered by the court or an agency?
Parents can determine the amount of child support by mutual agreement or by conclusion of court mediation.  In cases where they cannot reach an agreement, the court will decide the amount.
In cases where the amount of child maintenance is decided by judgment, the amount is calculated with a specific formula designed to reflect the concept that the parents are responsible for the life of their dependent child and the child should afford the same standards of living as the parents.
The relevant calculation tables are available on the website of the Tokyo Family Court ( (Hyperlink) ).  The tables show the outcome of the formular.
In the tables, the amount of child maintenance is calculated by using the information of yearly incomes of both parents.
Special elements, if any, will be taken into account by the court at the time of judgment in order to adjust the calculated amount to lead to a fair outcome.  For example, private school tuition fees would be one of such special elements that increase the monthly payment for the purpose of child support.
5.3       For how long is a parent required to pay child maintenance or provide financial support for their children? For example, can a child seek maintenance during university?
Though there is no provision stipulating the cut-off age of child support, in general, the parent is required to pay child maintenance until the child reaches the age of 20.
However, in cases where there are special circumstances that mean the child is unable to live by himself or herself, the parent has to pay child maintenance until the child is able to do so.
5.4       Can capital or property orders be made to or for the benefit of a child?
In cases where the parents cannot agree the amount of child maintenance, the court can decide the amount and make an order regarding child maintenance, but the court order will be the amount to be paid by the end of each month, not distribution of capital or property.
If agreed by the parents, flexible arrangements are possible, such as a capital payment to the custodial parent in lieu of future monthly payments.
5.5       Can a child or adult make a financial claim directly against their parents? If so, what factors will the court take into account?
If the child is represented by a statutory agent or is older than the age of 18, the child can make a financial claim against his or her parents.
In the court case, the judge will consider the need of the child in largely the same manner as cases where parents want the child maintenance for young children.  However, if the child is old enough, other factors relevant to the capacity of the child to earn money by himself or herself will be considered together with the best interests of the child and fairness of the payments in connection with the parents’ financial situation.

### 6. Children – Parental Responsibility and Custody

6.1       Explain what rights of custody both parents have in your jurisdiction whether (a) married, or (b) unmarried.
Under Japanese law, both parents have parental authority over children in wedlock during their marriage (Article 818(1) of the CC).  Parental authority includes both legal and physical custody.  At the time of divorce, by agreement or by the order of the court, one of the parents shall be given parental authority (Article 819(1) and (5) of the CC).
On the other hand, with regard to children out of wedlock, the mother has parental authority.
6.2       At what age are children considered adults by the court?
Under Article 4 of the CC, recently revised on April 1, 2022, the age of adulthood became 18.
6.3       What is the duration of children orders (up to the age of 16 or 18 or otherwise)?
A minor, which means any child under the age of 18, is subject to the parental authority of his or her parent(s) under Article 818(1) of the CC.  A female younger than 18 but older than 16 would not have been subject to parental authority if she had been married under Article 753 of the CC, under which she was regarded as an adult, but such Article was abolished on March 31, 2024, because of a revision to the age of marriage from 16 to 18 for females.
6.4       What orders can the court make in relation to children? Does the court automatically make orders in relation to child arrangements in the event of divorce?
Upon divorce, the court shall decide the sole parental authority if the parties to the divorce fail to determine who is to become the sole parental authority in accordance with Article 819(1) to (5) of the CC.
However, the New Civil Code will make it possible for the court to decide that joint custody will be granted to either both parents or one parent.
6.5       What factors does the court consider when making orders in relation to children?
There are various factors to consider when determining who should be awarded sole parental authority, the most important of which is “who has been the primary caregiver of the children”.  The court would prioritise the continuity of the child’s life.
However, if the child is older than 10 years old, the child’s own will or intention is a very important factor.
The future change of the primary caregiver and where the child will live tend to be deemed a huge psychological burden on the child’s well-being.  Therefore, the court tends to be hesitant to change the current caregiver of the child unless there are clear and strong reasons for the child’s well-being.
The factor of “being a friendly parent” is not established in Japan to determine sole parental authority.  Therefore, a parent who is not cooperative with visitation arrangements for the child’s well-being can often be awarded sole authority, which is a controversial topic among practitioners.
The factors normally considered are the following:
- The history of caregiving and the current caretaker.
- The future prospect of care.
- Time to be spent with the minors by the parent.
- The health of the parent.
- Financial stability.
- The will of the minors.
- The relationship with siblings.
- The bond with each parent.
The factors of the court after enforcement of the New Civil Code to grant joint custody to parents are currently unknown.  It is expected that the parents who can decide important issues for the child at least will be granted joint custody.
6.6       Without court orders, what can parents do unilaterally? For example, can they take a child abroad?
Under Japanese law, parental authority can be exercised without court order, and a parent who has sole parental authority or joint sole parental authority can take the child abroad without a court order.  Under the New Civil Code, it is stipulated that one parent will be able to exercise parental authority only when it comes to routine acts relating to care and education.
As to the abduction of the child, Japan is a party to the Hague Convention and, therefore, the return of the child may be ordered by the state where the child has been taken if one parent abducts the child and takes him or her abroad.
6.7       Is there a presumption of an equal division of time between separating or divorcing parents?
No.  Under current Japanese law, there is no concept of joint parental authority after divorce; however, as explained above, the New Civil Code will be different.
No presumption of an equal division of time between separating or divorcing parents is stipulated, even by the New Civil Code.
Equal division of time can be agreed by parents, but the court normally regards such arrangement to be burdensome on the child’s life.  The effect of the New Civil Code is unknow in this connection.
6.8       Are unmarried parents treated in the same way as married parents when the court makes orders on separation or divorce?
No.  Under Japanese law, unmarried parents are treated in a different manner.
Unmarried parents cannot have joint parental authority even if they want it.  Sole parental authority is normally held by the mother of the child, but the father can adopt the child and obtain the authority.
6.9       Is a welfare report prepared by an independent professional or is the decision taken by the judge alone? If so, does the child meet the judge?
A family court judge may order a family court evaluator to investigate the relevant facts of the case (Article 58(1) of the DRCPA).
The evaluator will report to the judge in writing after meeting the relevant child in connection with the visitation or custody.
6.10       Is there separate representation for children in your jurisdiction and, if so, who would represent them, e.g. a lawyer?
Yes.  Only a qualified lawyer can represent the child in accordance with Article 22(1) of the DRCPA.
6.11       Do any other adults have a say in relation to the arrangements for the children, e.g. step-parents or grandparents or siblings? What methods of dispute resolution are available to resolve disputes relating to children?
No.  Step-parents, grandparents or siblings do not have a say under Japanese law.
To resolve disputes relating to children, methods of family court mediation are available, but no court order can be expected in connection with the request of step-parents, grandparents or siblings for contact or custodial arrangements of the child.

### 7. Children – International Aspects

7.1       Can the custodial parent move to another state/country without the other parent’s consent?
Article 819(1) and (2) of the CC provide that one parent can have parental authority after the divorce, and such parent holding sole parental authority can decide the place of residence of the child (see Article 821 of the CC) and move to any place with the child without the consent of the other parent with no parental authority.
If the custodial parent is not yet divorced, he or she has no sole parental authority, and the place of residence of the child must be determined by parental agreements under Article 821 of the CC.  Furthermore, he or she needs to come to an agreement with or obtain the consent of the other parent to move to another country.
7.2       Can the custodial parent move to another part of the state/country without the other parent’s consent?
Please see the answer to question 7.1.  However, the visitation arrangement must be accomplished if such is based on a court order or an agreement.
7.3       If the court is making a decision on relocation of a child abroad, what factors are taken into account?
There is no legal procedure established under Japanese law where a court can render a relocation order, even if the relocation is outside Japan.  Therefore, there is no precedent.
7.4       If the court is making a decision on a child moving to a different part of the state/country, what factors are taken into account?
In Japan, there is no legal procedure where a court can render a relocation order, whether the relocation is within Japan or outside Japan.  Normally, the Japanese parent has sole parental authority and does not need permission from the court to move to a different part of the country.
7.5       In practice, how rare is it for the custodial parent to be allowed to relocate internationally/interstate?
In Japan, there is no legal procedure of a court order allowing the custodial parent to relocate the child internationally, nor where a parent can resolve any disagreement with the other parent, even for important issues such as children or custodial rights.
7.6       How does your jurisdiction deal with abduction cases? For example, is your jurisdiction a party to the Hague Convention?
Japan has been a party to the Hague Convention since April 1, 2014.
However, in Japan, child abduction by a parent within Japan is not prohibited and would not constitute a criminal action if it occurred at the time of parental separation.

### 8. Overview

8.1       In your view, what are the significant developments in family law in your jurisdiction in the last two years and anticipated in the next year?
The Ministry of Justice of Japan issued the Interim Proposal Concerning the Review of the Family Law on November 15, 2022.  The proposal was prepared by the Family Law Subcommittee of the Legislative Council.
In such proposal, it was mentioned that the terms of “parental authority” might be reviewed if better alternatives are found.  Therefore, it can be expected that the term will be amended dramatically to focus on parental authority for caregiving and the well-being of the child.
The first issue was the basic rule on parent-child relationships.  It was proposed that parents have a responsibility to parent a minor child and that they must consider the best interests of the child.  Such basic rule was not articulated in the current civil law of Japan.
The second issue, which was the most controversial, was the amendment of Article 819 of the CC as to parental authority after divorce.
Two options were proposed and the first one stated that this rule should be amended to allow both parents to have parental authority after divorce.  The second one stated that the current rule should remain unchanged.  The power of the custodial parent ( kango-sha ) was a big issue in this regard.
The third issue was the review of the rules concerning child custody arrangements after divorce.
In addition, for parents who are unable to agree on child maintenance, a new statutory system has been proposed so that a certain number of child support payments should be granted based on certain requirements.  The adoption system was subject to review as well.  Rules for distribution of property were discussed as to the period for filing a case and the disclosure system.
After the public comment procedure was completed between December 6, 2022 and February 17, 2023, and over 8,000 comments were received from a tremendous number of organisations and individuals, the proposed amendment to the Civil Code was discussed by the legal affairs committees of the House of Representatives and the House of Councillors before the amendment was finally enacted by lawmakers in May 2024.
The New Civil Code stipulates the following:
(1) Responsibility of parents
The responsibilities of parents to their child should be fulfilled in the best interests of the child, regardless of whether they are married.
Parents should respect a child’s personality for healthy psychological and physical development as well as each other’s personality so that they can cooperate with each other.
Currently there is no provision in the New Civil Code that stipulates that parents must cooperate with each other, and the above two main Articles seem very significant for parenting after divorce.
(2) Parental authority: who will hold it after divorce?
When the parents agree to divorce, they may also agree on whether parental authority should be exercised jointly or assigned to a parent.  If they cannot agree on parental authority, courts may decide joint or sole parental authority; however, the court must grant parental authority to one parent if the child’s interests would be harmed by granting joint parental authority.  This includes cases where:
Granting joint authority could possibly lead to abuse of the child or domestic violence physically or psychologically.
Joint parental authority is impractical, considering the circumstances.
As to changing the authority, the court shall take into account various factors, including how the parents reached their original agreement, to check that the agreement was made in the best interests of the child.
(3) Child support
In order to ensure child support payments, the New Civil Code will create new legal procedures in which a divorced parent (creditor) may:
Initiate the enforcement proceedings without a judicial decision that orders the debtor to pay, through liens.
Seek child support payments even when there is no agreement on the payments between the parents.
Make more efficient use of judicial procedures including application for enforcement and asset disclosure proceedings.
(4) Adoption
The new act will clarify rules concerning adoption and parental authority, such as:
Who has parental authority for an adopted child.
Requirements to adopt a child under 15.
(5) Distribution of property
Regarding distribution of property after divorce, the period is extended from two years to five years within which the divorced couple may seek it.  The court shall take into account various codified factors, including the parties’ contributions (which is presumed to be equal).
8.2       To what extent and how has the court process and other dispute resolution methods for family law been adapted in your jurisdiction in light of the COVID-19 pandemic – e.g. virtual hearings, remote access, paperless processes? Are any of these changes likely to remain now the COVID-19 crisis has passed?
During the COVID-19 pandemic, no substantial changes to the court/court mediation were seen, but court dates were limited compared to previous periods.  The courts merely cancelled court dates and mediation dates and began to utilise telephone mediation more regularly for parties living far away from the competent court.
Furthermore, Japanese courts recently started online court dates for civil litigation and divorce litigation in connection with preparatory proceedings.
These changes have been introduced permanently after reform of civil litigation law, and now it is generally possible for oral proceedings to be held online.
8.3       What are some of the areas of family law that you think should be considered in your jurisdiction, i.e. what laws or practices should be reformed?
Public support for, and enforcement of, visitation
Under Japanese law, visitation is not articulated as a right of children or parents, and the legal power of rights for visitation or contact is not established.  In addition, there is no public visitation centre or any public institution that can support contact or visitation of non-custodial parents in the long term.  Therefore, highly conflicted parents face serious difficulty in achieving stable visitation agreements for the well-being of children, and the court sometimes orders no visitation except allowing the custodial parent to send a few pictures every two or four months or even every year to the non-custodial parent.
Since no enforcement is systematised other than indirect enforcement, which is a sort of monetary sanction for payments during the period of the breach in limited situations, such as breach of a visitation agreement or order, visitation agreements or orders are often not properly complied with.  Certain reforms should be considered in this regard.
Domestic child abduction
There is no legal framework to ban domestic child abduction by a parent under current Japanese law.  Since only one parent can be awarded parental authority after divorce and all decision-making power will be held by him or her, custody battles during and before divorce are getting fiercer.
When considering bonds between parents and children, it is better that each parent upholds good communication with the child in a stable manner and to avoid custody battles at all costs.  However, the current Japanese parental legal system after divorce may worsen the battles, and an increasing number of parents are attracted to abducting their children since it is not prohibited.  Such abduction during a custody battle should be banned because they substantially harm the children.  The New Civil Code may prevent domestic child abduction, but the actual effect of the new law is unknown in connection with child abduction.
