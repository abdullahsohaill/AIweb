## Originality checker helps avoid all types of plagiarism

Basically, plagiarism occurs when one reuses any content from another source, such as a book or a website, and tries to pass it off as one's own without citing it properly.
If it is done intentionally, some use tricks to avoid verbatim copying - paraphrasing the text, adding hidden symbols, or using Cyrillic letters instead of similar Latin. Our plagiarism checker can indicate even the most intricate intentional plagiarism cases.
However, plagiarism can be occasional, when people just come to the same conclusions and express the idea in similar ways. Moreover, some students commit plagiarism by accident without any intent to cheat simply because they don't know that their sources need to be cited or how to cite them.
Still, that doesn't exempt them from responsibility, so learning how to reference materials correctly is essential. The originality checker can highlight the problem and prompt correct citations according to standardized formats.
It's worth saying that one type of reference can often go uncited in creative writing.
It's called an allusion, and it's implicit but still obvious enough for the audience to recognize it from another work. Here's an example. T. S. Eliot alluded to another poet, George Meredith, by repeating his line in the context of his poem Cousin Nancy: “The army of unalterable law.” Eliot expected his readers to recognize and attribute the line to its original author. He used it intentionally without trying to claim it as his own. In fact, poets love making allusions because they add intertextuality to their creations, making them seem more profound. In this case, the reference is not considered plagiarism but is often accompanied by a footnote.
